Since 1 April 2018, it has been unlawful to grant a new lease on a commercial property with an Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) rating below Band E. From 1 April 2023, this requirement was extended to cover all existing leases, meaning that any commercial property let with an EPC rating of F or G is now in breach of the regulations.

What is MEES?

The Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards (MEES) set minimum energy performance requirements for privately rented commercial and domestic properties in England and Wales. The EPC data used to assess compliance is drawn from the official MHCLG Energy Performance of Buildings register.

What is the proposed Band C target?

The UK government has signalled its intention to raise the minimum EPC requirement for commercial properties from Band E to Band C by 1 April 2028. This is a proposed regulatory target, it is not yet confirmed law as of March 2026.

However, the direction of travel is consistent with the government's broader net zero strategy and the Clean Growth Strategy. If enacted, the proposed Band C requirement would represent a significant step change from the current Band E minimum.

Which properties are affected?

Under the current regulations, only properties rated F or G are non-compliant. However, under the proposed 2028 Band C target, properties rated D, E, F, and G would all fall below the minimum standard.

A property rated D is perfectly compliant today, but if the proposed Band C target is enacted, that same property would become non-compliant. Centre for Cities analysis (2024) records around 70% of commercial floor space currently rated C or below.

What are the penalties?

Penalties for non-compliance with MEES are set out in SI 2015/962, Regulation 39. They are calculated based on the rateable value of the property and the duration of the breach. For breaches lasting three months or more, the penalty is 20% of the rateable value, with a minimum of £10,000 and a maximum of £150,000.

What should landlords do now?

  1. Get an up-to-date EPC for each property. Building conditions change, and an updated EPC gives you an accurate baseline.
  2. Understand the gap between your current band and Band C. A property rated D needs relatively modest improvements to reach C.
  3. Model retrofit costs against penalty exposure. In many cases, the cost of upgrading is significantly less than the cumulative penalty exposure.
  4. Prioritise properties with the largest gaps or highest rateable values. Properties with high rateable values face the largest penalties.
  5. Start planning retrofit works now. Contractor availability is becoming constrained as the proposed deadline approaches.
