1. Cyber Essentials v3.3 'Danzell'

The locked wording: Cyber Essentials v3.3 ('Danzell'), effective 28 April 2026, makes MFA gaps and 14-day patch SLA breaches auto-fails. v3.3 builds on the prior v3.2 release and tightens two of the historically-soft requirements: multi-factor authentication on cloud services, and the 14-day patching requirement on critical or high-severity vulnerabilities.

The IASME-administered scheme remains the route to certification, and an IASME-licensed Certifying Body is the only entity that can issue the certificate. This readiness check is a self-test, not a certification.

2. PPN 014 and the procurement angle

The locked wording: PPN 014 mandates Cyber Essentials for in-scope government, NHS, and MOD suppliers. If you are bidding for relevant central government, NHS, or MOD contracts and you are in-scope, Cyber Essentials is a contract-eligibility threshold, not a "nice-to-have". The readiness check tells you whether you are likely to clear that threshold.

3. The five technical themes

Cyber Essentials covers five technical themes:

  1. Firewalls: boundary firewalls and internet gateways at every internet-facing point.
  2. Secure configuration: default passwords replaced, unnecessary services disabled, secure baselines applied.
  3. User access control: account creation, removal, and least-privilege controls; MFA on cloud admin accounts.
  4. Malware protection: anti-malware on every device with executable code, plus enabled sandboxing for risky content.
  5. Security update management: patches applied within 14 days for critical/high-severity vulnerabilities.

The readiness check asks two to three questions per theme, weighted by impact on certification probability.

4. The two auto-fail axes

v3.3 introduces two auto-fail axes that override every other answer. If the user answers either of these positively, the check returns an auto-fail outcome and points to remediation:

  • MFA gap: any cloud service that holds organisation data, accessed by an administrator without MFA. This is the single biggest cause of v3.3 fails in industry data.
  • Patch SLA breach: any in-scope device with an unpatched critical/high-severity vulnerability older than 14 days, with no compensating control.

5. Scoring and traffic-light output

Each theme is scored Green / Amber / Red against the v3.3 control set. Output is a one-page PDF (when triggered) showing per-theme status and the prioritised remediation list. The check does not score on a scale of 0–100; that would imply false precision against a binary pass/fail certification process.

6. Scope considerations

The readiness check assumes whole-organisation scope by default. CE v3.3 allows a reduced scope (e.g. a specific business unit), but reduced scope is rarely sensible for SMEs and almost never for PPN 014-relevant suppliers. The full CrowCyber platform handles multi-scope evidence packs for groups certifying multiple legal entities.

7. What the check does not do

It does not run vulnerability scans against your infrastructure. It does not produce the formal Cyber Essentials questionnaire. It does not certify you. The full CrowCyber platform handles the full 44-question (CE) or 22-additional-question (CE+) self-assessment, evidence library with 12-month staleness tracking, and PPN 014 / 21 evidence packs.
