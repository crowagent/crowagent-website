How the Late Payment Calculator computes statutory interest

A full account of the Late Payment of Commercial Debts (Interest) Act 1998, the Bank of England base rate plus 8 percentage points, the tiered fixed-sum compensation, and the conditions under which the entitlement crystallises.

1. The source statute

The locked wording: Late Payment of Commercial Debts (Interest) Act 1998: statutory interest is Bank of England base rate + 8 percentage points; compensation tiered at £40 / £70 / £100 by invoice value. The Act was substantially amended by the Late Payment of Commercial Debts Regulations 2002 and 2013, but the headline framework is unchanged: a statutory entitlement to interest at base + 8 pp on commercial debts paid late, plus a fixed-sum compensation tier and recoverable reasonable costs.

2. When the entitlement crystallises

The entitlement applies to "commercial debts" — debts under business-to-business (or B2B and certain B2G) contracts for the supply of goods or services. It does NOT apply to consumer transactions.

The clock starts on the day after the agreed payment date (or, absent agreement, 30 days from the later of invoice receipt and goods/services delivery). The clock stops on the date the debt is paid in full.

3. The interest rate

Statutory interest is the Bank of England official base rate prevailing on the relevant six-month reference date, plus 8 percentage points. The reference dates are 30 June (for debts becoming overdue 1 July to 31 December) and 31 December (for debts becoming overdue 1 January to 30 June).

The Calculator looks up the appropriate base rate for the reference period covering the debt and applies the +8pp uplift. For debts straddling reference periods, the Calculator splits the period accordingly.

4. The fixed-sum compensation tiers

In addition to interest, the Act provides for fixed-sum compensation per debt:

  • £40 for debts under £1,000.
  • £70 for debts of £1,000 to £9,999.99.
  • £100 for debts of £10,000 and over.

Compensation is per debt, not per invoice. If a single contract generates multiple debts, each unpaid debt attracts its own fixed-sum compensation. Where reasonable recovery costs exceed the fixed-sum entitlement, the difference is also recoverable under section 5A(2A).

5. The Calculator's output

The Calculator produces a one-page itemised statement: invoice date, due date, payment date, days overdue, base rate applied, +8pp uplift, daily interest accrual, total interest, applicable fixed-sum tier, and total demand. Output is suitable to enclose with a payment-demand letter.

6. Edge cases

The Act does not apply to:

  • Consumer debts (B2C transactions).
  • Debts where the contract excludes the Act — subject to section 8 unfair-contract-term controls.
  • Debts arising under contracts with the Crown where the Crown is the supplier rather than the customer.

The Calculator flags these edge cases when the user inputs suggest a non-applicable scenario.

7. The waive-or-charge decision

It is a statutory entitlement, not a contractual one — you can choose to charge or waive. Many SMEs waive the entitlement to preserve customer relationships, then regret the decision when the debtor company subsequently fails. The Calculator does not advise; it produces a defensible figure if you choose to charge.

8. What the Calculator does not do

It does not chase the debt for you, draft a letter before action, generate a CCJ application, or interface with court systems. It produces the number. The full CrowCash platform extends to chase sequences (polite/standard/firm), AI payment-likelihood scoring, and audit-ready chase logs.
